# Layout & Composition Principles

## Overview

Layout is the structure of your design — how elements like text, buttons, and images are positioned.
Composition is the harmony between those elements — how they visually connect and guide users through the interface.

A good layout helps users find what they need. A strong composition makes them enjoy doing it.

## 5 Key Principles

### 1. Alignment

Alignment creates order and makes a design look intentional and professional.

- Use grid systems to maintain consistency.
- Align elements to a common baseline or edge.
- Avoid arbitrary positioning; every placement should be deliberate.

### 2. Proximity

Elements that belong together should stay close together.

- A form label and its input field should feel connected.
- Proximity helps users immediately understand relationships between components.
- Use spacing to separate unrelated groups.

### 3. White Space

Empty space is not wasted space. It is an active design element.

- Gives your design room to breathe.
- Helps focus user attention on what matters.
- Separates sections, improves readability, and adds a sense of calm.

### 4. Balance

Visual balance means distributing elements evenly so one side doesn't feel heavier than the other.

- A balanced layout feels natural and stable.
- An unbalanced layout can make users subconsciously uncomfortable.
- Balance can be symmetrical or asymmetrical, but it must be intentional.

### 5. Hierarchy

Not everything deserves equal attention.

- Use size, color, weight, and contrast to emphasize what matters most.
- The primary button should stand out more than a secondary action.
- Hierarchy guides the eye and makes the next step obvious.

## Why It Matters

Strong layout and composition:

- Make navigation effortless.
- Highlight key actions and information.
- Build trust and professionalism.
- Keep users engaged longer.

## Quick Tips

- Start with wireframes before adding color or visuals.
- Use grids to keep spacing consistent.
- Test different alignments; even small shifts change focus.
- Don't be afraid of empty space.
- Always step back and view your design as a whole.

## Relationship to Systematic Layout

These principles complement the systematic layout rules in the main workflow:

| Principle | Systematic Application |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Alignment | Use grid systems and consistent spacing (multiples of 8) |
| Proximity | Group related items with consistent internal padding |
| White Space | Apply margin and padding derived from the base value |
| Balance | Distribute elements using the spacing system and safe areas |
| Hierarchy | Combine size, contrast, and position (top/leading for importance) |
